When learning Spanish, one of the trickiest verbs for English speakers is "dar" ("to give"). At first glance, dar seems simple — it just means handing something over. But in real Spanish, especially in emotional expressions, dar is often used indirectly, almost like a reflexive emotion-giver. Understanding this subtle usage is key to sounding natural.

1. The Basic Structure of "Dar" in Emotional Expressions

Spanish uses this special structure:

[Indirect Object Pronoun] + [dar] + [emotion noun]

Example:
Me da miedo = "It gives me fear" → It scares me.

  • The thing causing the emotion is the subject (not you!).
  • The person feeling the emotion is the indirect object (me, te, le, nos, les).

You are receiving the feeling — the fear is "given" to you.

2. When to Use This Form of "Dar"

When translating from English to Spanish, you should use the "dar" structure if:

  • Something causes an emotional reaction in you.
  • The emotion is expressed as a noun (fear, pity, disgust, laughter, etc.).
  • You are the recipient of the emotion.

In these cases, Spanish prefers seeing the situation like:
"The thing is giving me [emotion]."

3. Common "Dar + Emotion" Combinations

Spanish Literal Meaning Natural English Translation
dar miedo to give fear to scare
dar risa to give laughter to make (someone) laugh
dar vergüenza to give shame to embarrass
dar pena to give pity to make (someone) feel sorry
dar asco to give disgust to gross out
dar lástima to give pity to make (someone) feel pity
dar coraje to give anger/courage to make (someone) mad
dar tristeza to give sadness to make (someone) sad
dar enojo to give anger to anger someone
dar alegría to give happiness to make (someone) happy

Notice that Spanish sees fear, shame, disgust, laughter, etc. as things given to you — not just things you "feel."

4. Real-Life Mini-Conversations Using "Dar"

Fear and Disgust

A: ¿Viste la película?
B: ¡Sí! Me dio miedo. ¡Esa escena final me dio asco!

Pity and Sadness

A: Cerraron el refugio de animales.
B: Me da mucha pena... y me da tristeza verlos en la calle.

Embarrassment

A: Me equivoqué en clase...
B: ¡Qué pena! Eso también me da vergüenza.

Laughter and Happiness

A: Ana imitando al profe siempre me da risa.
B: A mí me da alegría vernos de nuevo.

Anger

A: Siempre llegan tarde, me da coraje.
B: ¡Me da enojo que no respeten el tiempo!

5. How Spanish Texting Reflects These Emotions

In casual texting (especially in Mexico and Latin America), emotional expressions are often shortened:

Formal Spanish Texting Shortcut Meaning
Me da risa. Jaja me da risa / Jajaja Haha, it makes me laugh.
Me dio miedo. Qué miedo / Me dio miedito So scary!
Me da pena. Qué pena How embarrassing / What a shame.
Me da vergüenza. Qué oso So embarrassing!
Me da asco. Qué asco Gross!
Me da cosa. Me da cosita It makes me feel weird/awkward/cute.
Me da coraje. Qué coraje That makes me mad!

Extra Notes:

  • "Miedito" and "cosita" are cute diminutives.
  • "Qué oso" is slang for embarrassment in Mexico.
  • "Qué pena" is used broadly across Latin America.

Example texts:

  • Jaja me dio risa tu historia.
  • Uf qué miedo esa película.
  • Me dio cosa tocar eso.
  • Qué coraje perder el partido así.

6. Quick Recap

Concept Spanish Structure
Emotional cause (thing) Subject
Receiver of emotion (person) Indirect object (me, te, le, etc.)
Verb Dar (da/dan)
Emotion Noun (miedo, risa, asco, etc.)

If you're translating from English and the sentence involves:

  • Feeling caused by something,
  • And an emotional reaction that sounds like "makes me [feel]",

Use "dar + emotion noun" instead of simply using "ser" or "estar".

Final Tip:

Always think in Spanish like this:

"What is giving me the emotion?"

Instead of:

"I am feeling [emotion]."

This subtle shift in thinking will help you master natural-sounding Spanish — both in speaking and texting!
